## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Nate Herk's voice is best described as **competent-casual**: he has genuine authority (agency owner, community builder, real revenue numbers) but delivers it with zero pretension. His core rhythm is demo-first, explain-second. He accelerates through live builds and slows down only for concept explanations. Business/money videos are TIGHT-scripted with heavy credential and framework use; technical tutorials are LOOSE with mid-sentence corrections and "okay so" pivots. The universal CTA appears verbatim in almost every video from video 100 onward and is the single most consistent element in the entire channel.

**The one-sentence summary of his voice:** A no-code builder who made serious money and wants you to do the same, explaining everything as if he's showing a friend on his laptop.

## SECTION 3: ENERGY NOTES AND PACING

### 3A. The Acceleration/Deceleration Pattern

Nate's energy operates in two modes that alternate throughout every video:

**[accelerate]** — Used when:
- Starting a demo or live test ("so let's go ahead and throw a query")
- Revealing a result ("there we go," "look at that," "as you can see")
- Transitioning between steps in a build
- Hyping a feature or outcome
- Building toward the reveal in a Result-First video

**[slow]** — Used when:
- Introducing a concept for the first time
- Reading off credentials or agency revenue
- Explaining WHY something works (not HOW)
- The credential drop
- Framework enumeration (listing 5 steps takes longer per step)

Video 192 energy pattern is representative: "[accelerate] so the first thing to recognize is that we're chatting with our agent in nadn / [slows] so whether you've been building agents for a while now or this is going to be your first time taking a stab at it"

### 3B. Mid-Demo Pause Markers

When Nate is waiting for something to process, he narrates:
- "we'll see it take place real quick"
- "it's going to be hitting [tool] right now"
- "let's watch this happen"
- "just checking back in"
- "there we go"
- "while this is running, let's talk about…"
- "all right, while all of that's still getting set up, let's…" (V11)

These are practical transitions, not filler. They keep the viewer oriented during dead air in screenshares.

### 3C. The "Okay So" Reset

When Nate makes an error mid-build or needs to redirect: "okay so" — stated flatly, no embarrassment. Followed immediately by the correction or new direction. This is a signature LOOSE script moment.

## SECTION 14: SCRIPT TYPE PATTERNS

### 14A. TIGHT vs LOOSE

**TIGHT scripts:** Business advice, money videos, framework explainers, news/release videos
- Pre-structured with clear sections
- Credential drop is scripted and consistent
- Numbers memorized and quoted accurately
- No mid-sentence corrections
- Framework follows announced order
- Examples: V87 (PRICE framework), V92 (contrarian business hook), V163 ($240K in 6 months), V16 (Claude Code vs Antigravity), V30 (5 workflows businesses want), V26 (token hacks)

**LOOSE scripts:** Tutorial builds, screenshare demos, product reviews
- Off-the-dome while in the tool
- Mid-sentence corrections ("sorry, not post, I meant get")
- Live errors shown and debugged
- "okay so" resets are common
- Duration varies based on how the build goes
- Examples: V177 (deep research PDF), V180 (Jarvis build), V219 (personal assistant step-by-step), V8 (3D websites), V11 (24/7 trader), V19 ($10K trade challenge)

### 14B. The Hybrid Pattern (most common in mid/recent era)

Many videos have a TIGHT intro (scripted hook, credential) + LOOSE body (live demo) + TIGHT outro (CTA). This is the most common format and explains why the channel feels both polished and authentic.

### 14C. When Videos Are Fully TIGHT

Only for:
- "How I'd learn [X]" retrospective format (V82)
- Pure theory/concept explainers (V223 prompting masterclass, V227 AI agent masterclass)
- Business/sales videos with no demo component (V87, V92, V110, V120)
- News-and-release tier-list videos (V16, V13, V47, V35)
- Listicles like 32 tricks / 18 hacks / 5 tips (V3, V26)

### 14D. Indicators of LOOSE Script in Progress

- "um" and "like" frequency increases
- Double-correction phrases ("we're going to do — actually wait, let's do it this way")
- Real-time reading of tool outputs aloud
- "okay I think we need to — yeah" pivots
- "I wonder if" before testing a hypothesis
- "hmm, maybe I don't want to use the MCP server" (V4 — live decision-making narration)

### 16D. Type-Specific Templates

**TUTORIAL / DEMO (LOOSE, the most common format)**
- Hook: 1–2% (10–30 sec)
- Promise: 2% ("by the end of this video you'll have…")
- Credential: 0% (skip — just mention community plug mid-video)
- Concept: 4–6%
- Demo body: 85–90%
- CTA: 3%

**BUSINESS / MONEY VIDEO (TIGHT)**
- Hook: 2–4% (Result-First, dollar amount in first 15 sec)
- Promise: 3–4%
- Credential: 5–6% (placed within first 90 sec)
- Concept: 12–15%
- Body (framework + examples): 65–70%
- CTA: 4–5%

**COMPARISON / NEWS-RELEASE VIDEO (TIGHT)**
- Hook: 2–3%
- Promise: 3%
- Credential: 4% (often late, after demonstrating expertise via critique)
- Concept (what is the new tool): 25%
- Body (testing/comparison): 60%
- CTA: 3–5%

**COURSE / MASTERCLASS (TIGHT-LOOSE hybrid, very long)**
- Hook: 0.1–1% (the longer the course, the smaller the hook %)
- Promise: 0.5–1%
- Credential: 1%
- Concept (intro modules): 10–12%
- Body (deep tutorial): 83–87%
- CTA: 1–2%

### 16E. Pacing Rules of Thumb

1. **Hook should be 10–30 seconds (~30–80 words) for most videos.** Courses can have 5-second hooks. Business videos can stretch to 45 sec if the credential is folded into the hook.
2. **The credential drop is never the hook unless it's a money video.** If your hook is "I built X in 2 hours and got paid $Y," the credential is the hook.
3. **Tutorial body should be 75–90% of the video.** If your body is under 70%, you're either explaining too much theory or padding the intro.
4. **CTA should be 2–5% of the video, never more.** Nate's universal CTA is ~30 sec — that's 2% of a 25-min video.
5. **Concept/problem explanation scales inversely with viewer expertise.** Beginner videos spend 20–25% on concept; expert videos spend 5%.
6. **The longer the video, the smaller every non-body section becomes proportionally.** A 2-hour course can afford 1% on the hook because 1% is still 72 seconds. A 4-min short can't afford 1% because 1% is 2.4 seconds.
